Homework

Pulse (2016)


“Pulse” is my personal lament to the victims of the Pulse nightclub shooting happened on June 12, 2016 in Orlando, Florida. I was inspired to finally make this piece when I came a cross a news article saying that “First responders said the one thing that will haunt them the most about the Orlando terrorist attack is the constant sound of the victims’ cellphones ringing”. The sound of cell phone ringtones serves as a recurring motive in my piece – every ringtone represents an individual victim. I also did a research to look into all forty-nine victims’ lives, since I do think they should be remembered by how they lived. Based on the research, I found sound materials called “life snippets” that are relevant to the lives of some of the victims. Those life snippets are connected by ringtones as well. In the end, the ringtones are re-introduced by a heart monitor’s flat line in a more muffled, lamenting manner, and are concluded by the flat line, which evolves into a major chord, symbolizing hope. At the end, the heart monitor beeps forty-nine times as a tribute to the forty-nine victims. 
The piece devolves into partial chaos about two minutes before the coda because that part was composed around midnight of election day, when Donald Trump was already very close to being elected president. I was scared, confused and I was angry about the racism, sexism, homophobia and heteronormativity in this country and the rest of the world, about how innocent people are dying from hate crimes against minorities and how it still doesn’t wake people up. I also feared for my future in this country as a queer person of color. Therefore, this section of the piece features a lot of white noises, statics and poor signal reception sounds. Humanity is momentarily lost in this section as abstract, machinery noises take over. Although it might not sound like it, this is the section of the piece where I invested most of my emotions, which is also why I decided to keep it, even though it wasn’t as carefully crafted.  
However, I still decided to make the end hopeful - I decided to make the flat line evolve into a shimmery major chord and bring back the heart beats, the life. Not all hope is lost. Here’s to a better tomorrow.

Solar Wind (2016)


Solar Wind is an interactive system built in Max/msp that incorporates granular synthesis, delay, and filters. It is called “Solar Wind” because the resulting sound reminds me of electronically charged particles flowing in high speed. In this system, a contact microphone and a tilt sensor are attached to a hand-held percussive instrument (not necessarily an actual musical instrument), with the former getting sound from the instrument and passing it into the computer, and the latter constantly changing the parameters of the processing of the live input. The tilt sensor also provides values of resonance to a filter, which filters a fixed audio of ocean waves playing in the background to generate a wind effect. In contrast to the continuous, “legato”-like background sound, the processed live audio sounds short, percussive and “staccato”-ish. The delay gives some ringing to each separated notes to create a slight time-stretching effect without losing its staccato-ness, and because the tilt sensor is constantly updating the parameters of delaying, it also creates a subtle flanging effect. On the other hand, the granular synthesizer adds extra crispiness and pitch changes to the sound of the instrument, creating a crystal-like effect. Because of the high sensitivity of the tilt sensor, the result will sound random, but yet controlled. Such is the beauty of algorithmic composition. In this particular demo, I’m using a salad bowl with ping pong balls in it to demonstrate how this system turns everyday objects into musical instruments; I’m also using a board piano to show yet the extra charm it can add to pitched instruments.

The hardware part of this system includes one computer with Max/msp installed, one audio interface to connect the contact microphone to the computer, one midi controller, and E-Motion’s E-Mersion sensor system. The software part includes max/msp and eMotion_Basic. There are three inputs: the audio input from the contact microphone, the midi control input from the midi controller and the osc data input from the tilt sensor. The main components of the max/msp patch are delay, resonance and granular synthesis, with the objects tapin~, tapout~, reson~ and munger~. In the delay subpatch, there are three tapout~ objects with different delay times: short (50-100ms), medium(101-200ms) and long(201-400ms), and they’re respectively controlled by the GyroscopeX, GyroscopeY, and GyroscopeZ data from the tilt sensor. Similarly, in the resonance patch, there are three reson~ objects that handle low (130-260hz), medium (261-520hz), and high(521-1042hz) frequencies, respectively controlled by the AccelerometerX, AccelerometerY and AccelerometerZ data. Finally, in the granular synthesis patch, the grain separation, grain size, grain pitch and number of voices are respectively controlled by the MagnetometerX, MagnetometerY, MagnetometerZ and orientation data. The midi controller only controls the faders in the mixer patch to create smoother fades. I decided to incorporate it to practice my midi parsing and also for the sake of the quality of the demo. Finally, there is also a sfrecord~ object in the patch to record the awesome sounds this instrument makes.
